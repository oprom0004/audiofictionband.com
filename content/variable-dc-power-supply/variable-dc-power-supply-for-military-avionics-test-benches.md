## Critical Specifications for Avionics Simulation

Testing avionics components requires more than just a power source; it requires an instrument capable of replicating the dynamic electrical environment of an aircraft. When selecting a power supply for these applications, engineers must prioritize several key technical parameters.

### Ripple Noise and Stability
Avionics components are notoriously sensitive to electromagnetic interference (EMI) and power supply noise. A high-quality variable DC power supply must maintain minimal ripple and noise to ensure that the DUT (Device Under Test) is not affected by the power source's own switching characteristics. The ETM series features advanced filtering circuitry to ensure clean power delivery, protecting the integrity of high-frequency signal processing within the avionics stack.

### CC/CV Crossover and Response Time
In military applications, power supplies often encounter complex load profiles, including capacitive startup inrush and inductive transients. The transition between Constant Current (CC) and Constant Voltage (CV) modes must be seamless to prevent damaging overshoots. Furthermore, rapid transient response times are essential for simulating abrupt power bus fluctuations. The ETM-8005 and ETM-8006 units are designed to handle these transitions with minimal latency, ensuring that the test environment remains stable even under aggressive load cycling.

## Integrating Advanced Compliance Standards

Modern avionics testing is increasingly influenced by broader power distribution standards, even in specialized military sectors. As test benches evolve to incorporate higher power levels and more sophisticated control logic, referencing established guidelines—such as the [U.S. DOE EV Charging Infrastructure Report](https://www.energy.gov/eere/vehicles/electric-vehicle-charging)—can provide valuable insights into standardized testing and grid-interface reliability. While avionics operate on distinct standards (such as MIL-STD-704), the methodologies for ensuring voltage sag immunity, such as those discussed in SEMI F47 compliance, remain highly applicable for verifying the robustness of onboard power electronics.

AFB Power’s ETM series supports these requirements through sophisticated remote sensing capabilities. By utilizing remote sense, engineers can compensate for voltage drops across long cable runs often found in expansive test benches, ensuring that the voltage delivered to the avionics unit is exactly as programmed, regardless of current draw.
